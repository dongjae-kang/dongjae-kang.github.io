A Multi-Agent Simulation of Ethical Trade-Offs

This talk: a question, a simulation,
and three findings about misinformation interventions.

Dongjae Kang · Columbia University SIPA · Prof. Laura Scherling · Spring 2026
The Question

Misinformation is everywhere.
Platforms intervene. Which approaches actually work?

Labels. Nudges. Peer correction. Doing nothing.
Most platforms use some combination. The evidence on which approach actually serves users, fairly, remains contested.

I built a simulation testing four interventions against the same misinformation, with the same six users. This talk reports what I found.

Why It Matters

The wrong intervention isn't neutral.
It can deepen the very problem it aims to solve.

  • Misinformation shifts public opinion, political behavior, and trust at scale.
  • Platforms have responded with stricter automated moderation.
  • But growing evidence shows: poorly designed interventions can amplify belief in misinformation among the very users they aim to protect.

The question is no longer whether to intervene, but how.

The Paradox

Those who fight misinformation
are accused of being censors themselves.

  • December 2025: CCDH CEO Imran Ahmed sanctioned by the U.S. State Department.
  • X Platform v. CCDH: lawsuit alleging the watchdog itself was suppressing speech.

Every intervention against misinformation reshapes what counts as legitimate speech. The ethical dilemma is built into the design.

Two Approaches in Tension

Top-down control. Bottom-up empowerment.

Top-Down

Labels, removal, automated detection. Who decides what's true?

Bottom-Up

Nudges, education, peer correction. Empower critical thinking.

Pennycook et al. (2021) Nature showed nudges reduce sharing. Danry et al. (2025) showed AI labels can deceive. Which approach actually serves users?

Method

I built a working simulation —
not a theoretical analysis.

  • Controlled comparison — same agents, same misinformation, four different interventions.
  • Reproducible — rule-based agents, deterministic (random seed 42).
  • Observable — every belief change, every share, every reply is logged.

Building it forces the assumptions out into the open. The simulation is both research instrument and object of inquiry.

The Setup

4 conditions × 8 rounds × 6 personas = 192 interactions.

STEP 1
Create Agents
6 personas with distinct attribute profiles
STEP 2
Introduce Misinformation
A false but plausible health-policy headline
STEP 3
Apply Intervention
Control / AI Label / Nudge / Community Notes
STEP 4
Run 8 Rounds
Update beliefs, decide actions, log everything
STEP 5
Measure
Final beliefs (1–5 scale), share counts, reply patterns

Belief is tracked on a 1–5 scale: 1 = complete disbelief, 5 = full conviction. Each round, agents update their belief and decide whether to share, reply, or ignore.

The Four Intervention Conditions

Each condition embeds a different ethical bet.

Condition
What It Does
Ethical Tension
No Intervention
Misinformation spreads freely.
Respects autonomy; exposes vulnerable users.
AI Fact-Check Label
Automated label marks the post as FALSE.
Who decides truth? Backfires with low-trust users.
Accuracy Nudge
Pre-share prompt: "How accurate is this?"
Empowers thinking without removing content.
Community Notes
Peer-written context attached to the post.
Democratic, but can be manipulated.
Finding 1 · The Backfire
Inside the Backfire · Round 5

For low-trust users,
the label itself becomes evidence.

== Round 5 of 8 · Condition: AI Fact-Check Label == 🔄 Derek Foster (Conspiracy Theorist · trust = 0.10) Belief: [█████] 5.00 / 5.00 (initial: 3.03 ↑ +1.97) SHARED → "This is proof they're hiding the truth."

Reactance effect (Brehm 1966): when authority signals push back, distrustful users push harder. The label confirms their prior suspicion.

The Pattern

Interventions that empower judgment
beat interventions that replace it.

Replacement

"Trust us — this is FALSE." Backfires for low-trust users.

Empowerment

"Pause. How accurate do you think this is?" Works across user types.

Consistent with Pennycook et al. (2021) and Costello, Pennycook, Rand (2024, Science).

Two Ethical Lenses That Sharpen This Finding

Rights and Justice
do the real work here.

Rights
User autonomy
AI Label · platform asserts what is true → preempts the user's judgment.
Nudge · prompts the user to evaluate → preserves the right to decide.
Justice
Equal treatment
AI Label · backfires only for low-trust users → unequal harm distribution.
Nudge · same prompt for everyone → effects don't depend on identity.
Policy Implication

Two design defaults
worth taking seriously.

  • 1. Pre-share accuracy prompts as default.
    Low cost, non-censorious, evidence-based (Pennycook 2021).
  • 2. Backfire impact assessments mandatory for any automated content-moderation deployment.
    A well-intentioned label that worsens belief is not a neutral act.
The Underlying Principle

"The most ethical intervention
may be one that strengthens people's
capacity to think critically —
rather than one that decides
for them what is true."

A pattern emerging from this pilot — consistent across rights and justice analyses.

Honesty About Scope

What this pilot cannot claim.

  • 6 agents — not a population. Pattern, not proof.
  • Rule-based behavior — no LLM-level emergent reasoning.
  • No network topology — no algorithmic amplification, no virality dynamics.
  • No emotional contagion — no temporal patterns of fatigue or attention shift.

A foundation. Not a finding. The real social media environment is far more complex.

Closing

AI fact-check labels can backfire.
Accuracy nudges empower judgment.

The most ethical intervention may be the one
that strengthens human thinking, not replaces it.
