Microsoft Pledged to Save Water. In the A.I. Era, It Expects Water Use to Soar.

Driven by the artificial intelligence frenzy, Microsoft is internally projecting that water use at its data centers will more than double by 2030 from 2020, including in places that face shortages.

In 2020, Brad Smith, Microsoft's president, made a sweeping promise: the company would become "water positive" by the end of the decade, replenishing more water than it consumed across its global operations. The pledge was part of a broader sustainability campaign, positioning Microsoft as an environmental leader among tech giants racing to build artificial intelligence.

But internal documents obtained by The New York Times tell a starkly different story. Microsoft's own projections, detailed in planning materials covering roughly 100 data center complexes worldwide, show the company expects to consume 28 billion liters of water annually by 2030 — more than triple the 7.9 billion liters it used in 2020. The surge is driven almost entirely by the enormous computing power required to train and run A.I. systems, which generate far more heat than traditional server workloads and demand significantly more water for cooling.

When The Times contacted Microsoft for comment, the company initially asked for additional time to respond. Weeks later, it provided a substantially revised set of projections, claiming that new cooling technologies and efficiency measures would reduce its 2030 water consumption to 18 billion liters. However, the revised figures excluded water usage from $50 billion in new data center deals that the company has recently announced, meaning the actual total would likely be far higher.

Priscilla Johnson, who served as Microsoft's water strategy director from 2017 to 2020, said that environmental concerns had long taken a back seat to financial calculations. "Water was very much an afterthought," she said. "Energy was more the focus because it was more expensive. Water was too cheap to be prioritized."

The consequences of that calculus are now playing out across the globe. In Jakarta, one of the world's most water-stressed cities, Microsoft's internal projections called for consuming 1.9 billion liters annually at its planned data center facility — a figure the company later revised to 664 million liters. Jakarta, home to more than 10 million people, is sinking at one of the fastest rates of any major city, in part because of groundwater extraction. The city's water infrastructure already fails to reach millions of its residents.

In Phoenix, where two decades of drought have left the region's reservoirs at historically low levels, Microsoft projected consuming 3.3 billion liters per year — later revised to 2 billion. The Colorado River, which supplies much of the region's water, has been the subject of increasingly fraught negotiations between states that depend on it. Adding billions of liters of industrial consumption to an already strained system has alarmed local officials and environmental groups.

The scale of water use in data centers is often underappreciated. About two-thirds of the cooling water consumed by these facilities evaporates into the atmosphere and cannot be recaptured. And Microsoft's published figures, like those of most major technology companies, exclude the vast quantities of water consumed by the power plants that generate the electricity data centers require. When that indirect water use is counted, the true footprint is significantly larger.

"We remain committed to reducing water use and replenishing water sources in the communities where we operate," said Melanie Nakagawa, Microsoft's chief sustainability officer. She said the company was investing in advanced cooling systems and water restoration projects, though she declined to provide a specific timeline for achieving the "water positive" goal.

The tension between Microsoft's sustainability commitments and its A.I. ambitions reflects a broader pattern across the technology industry. Nationally, U.S. data center water consumption is projected to grow from roughly 60 billion liters in 2022 to between 150 and 275 billion liters by 2028, according to estimates from the U.S. Department of Energy. The range is wide because so much about the industry's expansion remains unknown.

Few disclosure rules exist for data center water consumption. Unlike power plants or manufacturing facilities, technology companies in the United States face no federal requirement to report how much water their data centers use. Some states have begun to push for greater transparency, but the industry has largely been left to regulate itself. The result is a patchwork of voluntary disclosures that experts say significantly understates the true scope of the problem. As A.I. becomes embedded in daily life — from search engines to medical diagnostics to financial trading — the environmental costs of the infrastructure behind it are only beginning to come into focus.
