Beyond Binary

A granular AI disclosure system for professional networking, prototyped from focus group findings.

Alexandra Bilinkis · Bining Yang · Clare Elwell · Dongjae Kang · Jingyi Fu · Sara Shibata
TPINIA7006 Digital Content Provenance · Prof. Mounir Ibrahim · Columbia SIPA · Spring 2026

The problem this prototype responds to

Our focus group with six HR and recruiting professionals found that the binary “Generated by AI” label does not behave as a transparency signal in professional networking contexts. Instead, it acts as a leveling cue: when present, viewers cannot meaningfully distinguish real photos from AI-generated ones, and engagement rates converge to a similar middle band regardless of the underlying truth.

Reply rate on LinkedIn (focus group, n = 30 ratings per cell)
Real photo AI-generated photo
No label 83.3% 60.0%
Labeled “Generated by AI” 73.3% 73.3%

This prototype demonstrates how a more granular label vocabulary, combined with content credentials, could restore informed differentiation while preserving transparency.

Compare the two systems

Toggle between the current binary label and our proposed granular system. The eight profile cards below are identical in both modes. Only the disclosure layer changes.

In the current system, half of the cards carry a single “Generated by AI” label. Two of those are actually authentic photos that were mislabeled. Try to tell which. In the proposed system, every card declares one of four provenance tiers, with content credentials available on click.

All eight images in this prototype are AI-generated (source: thispersondoesnotexist.com). Tier assignments below illustrate how the proposed system would categorize different provenance scenarios in a real deployment.

Why this matters

  1. Granular beats binary.

    A single “Generated by AI” label collapses minor edits and full fabrication into one undifferentiated category. Four tiers preserve the information viewers need to make context-appropriate judgments.

  2. Color and shape carry meaning faster than text.

    Hiring teams scan many profiles quickly. A small corner mark, paired with a one-word tier name, conveys provenance in the time it takes to read a job title.

  3. Content credentials make the signal verifiable.

    The provenance chain on each card mirrors the C2PA open standard: a checkable record of capture, edits, and AI involvement, rather than a self-reported claim.

About this prototype

Built as Appendix A to the team paper Digital Content Provenance: A Focus Group Study on AI Labels and Professional Identity Trust (Bilinkis, Yang, Elwell, Kang, Fu, & Shibata, 2026). The prototype is a static page; no data is collected from visitors.

Image source. All portraits are AI-generated samples from thispersondoesnotexist.com, consistent with the base-image methodology used in our focus group stimuli. No real persons are depicted.
